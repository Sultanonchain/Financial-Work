Methodology

Every assumption VALUS makes when pricing a stock, in detail. Educational only — not investment advice.

DCF model

VALUS uses a two-stage discounted free cash flow model with a 10-year explicit projection window and a Gordon growth terminal value. The base equation: 

IV = Σ (FCF_t / (1+WACC)^t) for t=1..10
   + (FCF_10 × (1+g_term) / (WACC − g_term)) / (1+WACC)^10
   + Net cash on balance sheet

Each year's projected free cash flow grows at a stage-specific rate (stage 1 = years 1–5, stage 2 = years 6–10), discounted back to today using WACC, then summed.

Free cash flow input

VALUS uses trailing free cash flow as reported by Yahoo Finance (operating cash flow minus capital expenditure). For companies with volatile FCF, the model uses a smoothed average across the most recent reported years rather than a single noisy data point.

WACC (discount rate)

The weighted average cost of capital combines the company's after-tax cost of debt and its cost of equity (estimated via CAPM). Defaults to a sector-typical value when components aren't reliably available. Users can override WACC interactively from the Customize panel.

Terminal growth

Capped at 3% by default — the long-run rate at which a mature company can grow without exceeding global GDP growth indefinitely. Adjustable from 1% to 5% in the Customize panel.

Sector-aware growth ceilings

A semiconductor company can plausibly compound at 18% for a few years; a regulated utility cannot. VALUS caps stage 1 and stage 2 growth rates at sector-specific ceilings, derived from observed long-term growth distributions. This prevents the model from extrapolating one good year into a fantasy outcome — the most common DCF failure mode.

Reverse DCF (implied growth)

In addition to the forward DCF, VALUS solves for the constant growth rate that would make intrinsic value equal the current price — the "implied growth" the market is pricing in. If a stock trades at a price requiring 25% annual growth for a decade and the sector ceiling is 12%, the verdict tier flags it as "Priced for Miracle." Reverse-DCF is the single most useful sanity check on whether today's price is plausible.

Confidence flags

VALUS surfaces low-confidence warnings when the inputs are unreliable: negative or volatile FCF, missing share count, recent IPO with limited history, or financials in a currency yfinance can't fully normalize. Treat the IV with extra skepticism in those cases.

Verdict tier assignment

After computing margin of safety, VALUS bins the result into one of seven verdict tiers (Distress through Miracle). For a small set of pre-revenue strategic assets (defense primes, CHIPS Act recipients, DOE-backed projects), VALUS applies a "Strategic Discount" override: pure DCF undervalues these because earnings haven't materialized, but sovereign-backstopped franchises have a credible path the cashflow model can't see.

Reality reconciliation

After the raw DCF runs, VALUS blends the result with consensus analyst price targets where they exist, weighted by data quality. This dampens cases where a single noisy FCF data point would otherwise swing IV wildly.
